LTSpice Power Electronics CubeSat Hardware Validation
84.8% power conversion efficiency 33mV (1%) voltage undershoot ~20µs settling time 1.5A load validated

As part of the Orbital CubeSat Electrical Team at the University of Waterloo, I developed a high-fidelity LTSpice digital twin of a synchronous buck converter. The goal was to fully characterize performance before committing to PCB layout — a standard practice in professional hardware development that prevents costly spins.

The simulation was designed to validate three key performance areas: steady-state efficiency, transient response, and feedback loop stability. Achieving 84.8% power conversion efficiency under a 1.5A load demonstrated that the converter topology and component selection were well-optimized for the CubeSat's power budget.

The transient analysis was particularly important. By applying a heavy load step and monitoring the output voltage, I quantified a minimal 33mV (1%) voltage undershoot — well within the tolerance required for sensitive digital loads — with a rapid ~20µs recovery settling time. This data directly informed the output capacitor selection and feedback compensation network design.

The LTSpice model served as the definitive reference for the subsequent PCB design, ensuring the manufactured board would meet performance specs before the first prototype was assembled.

Key Technologies

  • LTSpice XVII: Full switching-mode power supply simulation — switching transients, efficiency sweeps, AC loop analysis
  • Feedback Compensation: Type II/III compensator design to achieve adequate phase margin and transient recovery
  • Load Transient Analysis: Step-load testing methodology to characterize dynamic voltage regulation
  • Component Parametric Sweeps: Sensitivity analysis across inductor DCR, MOSFET RDS(on), and output capacitance
