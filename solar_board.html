Executed manual assembly and soldering of a solar power PCB for the Orbital CubeSat team. Validated physical hardware performance metrics against LTSpice simulation models, closing the loop between digital design and physical reality.

PCB Assembly CubeSat LTSpice Soldering
Solar power PCB assembled Sim vs. hardware validated Orbital CubeSat team

As part of the Orbital CubeSat Electrical Team, I was responsible for the physical assembly and testing of a solar cell power PCB — one of the critical subsystems in the satellite's power distribution chain.

The assembly process involved fine-pitch SMD soldering of the solar cell interface components, bypass capacitors, protection diodes, and test points. CubeSat hardware demands extremely high reliability — every solder joint was inspected under magnification to ensure it met IPC-A-610 acceptability standards.

Once assembled, the board was characterized using bench power supplies and a solar cell simulator. The measured performance data — open-circuit voltage, short-circuit current, maximum power point — was then compared directly against the LTSpice simulation model developed earlier in the project cycle. This simulation-to-hardware validation process confirmed the design was performing as expected and identified a minor discrepancy in parasitic series resistance that was subsequently corrected in the next revision.

This project reinforced the importance of the hardware-in-the-loop validation discipline — simulations are only as good as their models, and real hardware always reveals something new.

Key Technologies

  • SMD Soldering: Fine-pitch component assembly with hot-air rework station and temperature-controlled iron
  • LTSpice Verification: Cross-referenced measured I-V curves against simulation model predictions
  • Bench Characterization: Source/measure unit used for I-V curve tracing and MPP determination
  • Visual Inspection: IPC-A-610 solder joint quality assessment under 10x magnification
